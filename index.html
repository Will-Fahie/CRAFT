Space has no supply chain

Building a space station today is like shipping a skyscraper in a moving truck. One piece at a time, custom-built on Earth, tested for years, launched nearly empty. Adding pressurized volume takes 5+ years and tens of thousands of dollars per cubic meter.

As commercial space matures — private stations, research labs, agricultural modules, fuel depots, mining crew quarters — the current model of bespoke Earth-built modules cannot scale. Every new room requires a new mission.

Space needs what every industrial frontier eventually needs: local manufacturing capacity.

The orbital foundry

We stockpile bulk feedstock in orbit and manufacture on-demand. C.R.A.F.T. 3D-prints structural shells, lines them with airtight membranes, and produces airlocks — all in-situ.

Habitats are the wedge. Once the foundry exists, it produces whatever the space economy needs next: storage silos, docking adapters, vehicle components, replacement parts. As asteroid mining and lunar industry come online, we become the industrial backbone.

From exploration to industry

We give space its first supply chain. New stations get built in months instead of decades. Mining operations scale capacity without waiting on Earth. Space moves from an exploration era to an industrial era.

C.R.A.F.T. becomes the primary manufacturing base for LEO, the Moon, and Mars.

Starting with habitats

On-demand habitat modules sold to stations needing expansion, new commercial entrants building their first outpost, or mining operations scaling faster than Earth can deliver. Habitats are the wedge into a much larger platform.
